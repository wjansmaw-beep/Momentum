# Experience Promises

Status: Directional  
Version: 1.0

## Definition

An Experience Promise is the smallest truthful expression of why an experience could be worth choosing now. It turns contextual fit into felt possibility without becoming advertising or a guarantee.

> Feeling → Specific detail → Why now → Action

Not every promise needs four visible sentences. The structure is a writing test; the interface may compress it.

## Why it works

People often feel desire before evaluating practical proof. Momentum therefore shows the likely experience first, then provides compact feasibility and an optional “Why this fits.” This reduces decision effort while keeping reasoning inspectable.

The promise must never hide cost, risk, distance, accessibility, or uncertainty that would materially affect the choice.

## Writing rules

1. Lead with a human outcome or a vivid future moment.
2. Use one concrete sensory or meaningful detail.
3. Explain immediacy only with verified context.
4. Make the first action specific and easy.
5. Match certainty to evidence: confirmed, likely, or possible.
6. Prefer one strong promise over a list of benefits.
7. Avoid commands, generic inspiration, superlatives, health guarantees, and invented urgency.
8. Do not expose sensitive context in the promise.

## Pattern

**Primary line:** a future the user can picture.  
**Wonder detail:** the distinctive reason it matters.  
**Feasibility:** time, distance, conditions, or preparation.  
**Action:** language that begins this particular experience.  
**Why this fits:** optional, secondary, and correctable.

## Examples

### Walking and running

- “In 30 minutes you could have wind and open water around you. The path is quiet before sunset.” → **Take me there**
- “A short run may give you the clear head you were looking for. This route fits the 25 minutes you have.” → **Start this run**
- “The rain has stopped and the streets are nearly empty. A gentle loop could help the workday fall away.” → **Walk this loop**

### Workouts

- “You have enough time for a focused strength session that should leave you capable, not depleted.” → **Set up the workout**
- “Twelve minutes, one kettlebell, and a clear finish. This could be the small win your afternoon needs.” → **Begin the first set**
- “Your recent activity suggests keeping today light. Move gently and finish feeling looser.” → **Start mobility**

### Breathing and restoration

- “Five quiet minutes could help your body settle before sleep.” → **Begin breathing**
- “Nothing needs to be achieved right now. This short pause is designed to lower the pace of the evening.” → **Take the pause**
- “You may need recovery more than another task. Put the phone down after this two-minute reset.” → **Guide me briefly**

### Food and recipes

- “In 15 minutes you could be eating something warm, fresh, and familiar. You already have most ingredients.” → **Cook this tonight**
- “Your favorite shake fits the morning you have: quick, cold, and ready before you leave.” → **Make my shake**
- “The local market has fresh strawberries today. This simple dessert makes them the part you remember.” → **See what I need**

### Nature, birds, and Hidden Gems

- “The wind is expected to fall near sunset, and the sea aster is in bloom. In 45 minutes you could be at a quiet viewpoint over the water.” → **Bring me there**
- “A sea eagle was reported here this morning. It may be gone, but the observation point is still worth the light and silence.” → **Prepare this visit**
- “Five minutes beyond the popular path is a bench most people pass. Tonight it faces the last light.” → **Show the route**

### Family and connection

- “You have an open hour together. This nearby place gives you something to discover without turning it into a project.” → **Let’s go**
- “Ten undistracted minutes may matter more than a long plan. Choose one question and call someone you miss.” → **Give me the question**
- “Cook this together: one person chops, one person mixes, and dinner becomes the memory.” → **Start together**

### Travel and culture

- “A small photography exhibition closes in two hours, ten minutes from where you are staying. One room is devoted to the coast you explored yesterday.” → **Take me there**
- “The main square is busy; one street behind it, a local trio starts at eight.” → **See the details**
- “You do not need a full itinerary. This one viewpoint could define the afternoon.” → **Plan the short route**

### Learning and curiosity

- “One idea, fifteen focused minutes, and something useful to try tomorrow.” → **Open the lesson**
- “This painting is easy to walk past. Give it three minutes before reading its story.” → **Find it**

## Weak-to-strong examples

- Weak: “Go for a healthy walk.”  
  Stronger: “The rain has cleared. Twenty quiet minutes by the water could help your head feel spacious again.”
- Weak: “Recommended because your recovery is high.”  
  Stronger: “You seem ready for a short, demanding set—and you have just enough time to finish without rushing.”
- Weak: “Amazing hidden gem nearby!”  
  Stronger: “A sheltered bench overlooks the old harbor, seven minutes from here. Late light reaches it around eight.”

## Validation checklist

- Is the desired feeling plausible rather than promised as fact?
- Is the distinctive detail sourced and fresh when time-sensitive?
- Is “why now” relevant rather than manufactured?
- Can the user see the practical commitment before accepting?
- Does the action enter an Experience Capsule instead of more browsing?
- Would the wording still feel respectful after the user declines?
