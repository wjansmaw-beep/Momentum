# Meaning, Goals, and Guided Learning

Status: Directional  
Version: 1.0

## Purpose

Momentum should not only help someone begin an activity. It may help them understand what they are doing, notice more of the world, and connect an experience to a direction that matters to them.

This does not create a separate Purpose module or a productivity dashboard. Meaning, goals, and learning are quiet layers inside Understanding, the Experience Promise, the Capsule, and Memory.

> Momentum can suggest the experience, explain just enough to deepen it, and help the person notice why it mattered.

## Three horizons of personal direction

Momentum separates three different forms of direction. They must not be collapsed into one score or inferred as identity.

### Near intentions

What the person consciously wants more room for in the coming days or weeks.

Examples:

- sleep with a calmer head this week;
- cook at home twice;
- move during workdays;
- make time with a child;
- explore the place where I currently live or travel.

Near intentions may influence timing and candidate ranking. They remain editable and should expire or be reviewed rather than becoming permanent assumptions.

### Growth directions

Capabilities, habits, or themes someone wants to develop over months.

Examples:

- become stronger;
- learn to recognize local birds;
- understand cooking techniques;
- become more comfortable exploring alone;
- learn more about local history and culture.

Growth directions help Momentum create continuity across otherwise separate experiences. They are not quotas.

### Meaning anchors

The values, roles, relationships, places, or forms of contribution that the person explicitly says matter.

Examples:

- being a present parent or partner;
- caring for health without making life revolve around measurement;
- curiosity, nature, craftsmanship, service, faith, creativity, or community;
- preserving meaningful family memories;
- contributing knowledge or attention to others.

Meaning anchors should be user-authored or explicitly confirmed. Momentum must not infer a worldview, religion, relationship quality, or life purpose from behavior.

## How direction affects suggestions

Direction is one contextual influence, never a command. Candidate selection should still respect the Human Moment, explicit present intent, time, feasibility, safety, repetition, and the living world.

A suggestion may explain the connection in ordinary language:

- “You wanted to learn more about the birds around you.”
- “This fits the strength you are gradually building.”
- “You wanted to make more room for unhurried time together.”

Momentum should not say:

- “You failed your weekly goal.”
- “This is what your life needs.”
- “You should choose this because you said family matters.”

The person can ignore, pause, edit, or remove every direction without penalty.

## Guided learning is part of the experience

Learning in Momentum is not limited to recommending a book, article, or course. Momentum can provide a small, complete learning experience when it has trustworthy information and the moment supports attention.

The preferred pattern is:

> one relevant idea -> one thing to notice or do -> one optional trace to remember

The learning should be situated. It appears because it improves the experience that is already happening, not because the system wants more engagement.

### Place and route guidance

A walk through Dokkum, New York, or another place may include a small number of location-relevant guide moments:

- a historical event connected to the street or building in view;
- why the city, landscape, water, architecture, or local custom looks the way it does;
- a cultural story or work of art that becomes visible at that point;
- a respectful question that invites the person to look rather than stare at the phone.

Guidance should be sparse, timed safely, and available as short audio where appropriate. Navigation remains with the trusted navigation capability. Momentum never invents local history or silently presents generated synthesis as a verified fact.

### Nature guidance

A nature experience may explain:

- which birds or plants may plausibly be present and how to recognize them;
- migration, bloom, tide, season, weather, light, or habitat;
- which observable detail distinguishes one species from another;
- how to avoid disturbing wildlife or fragile places.

Live observations remain evidence of a recent observation, not a guarantee. Sensitive species and locations stay protected.

### Workout guidance

A workout may explain, in proportion to the moment:

- the purpose of the movement pattern;
- which capability the session is designed to develop;
- one essential technique cue;
- why rest, load, order, or tempo is being used;
- a safe alternative when equipment or capacity changes.

This is education, not diagnosis or individualized medical treatment. The Capsule prioritizes doing safely over reading anatomy.

### Recipe and food guidance

A recipe may explain:

- what an ingredient contributes to taste, texture, structure, or nourishment;
- why a cooking technique changes the result;
- which substitution preserves the function of the ingredient;
- relevant cultural or regional context when it is accurately sourced;
- how the person’s own adjustment changed the outcome.

Health-adjacent explanations must remain evidence-calibrated and may not turn a meal into a moral score.

### Interest-led learning

When a user is interested in a topic, Momentum may create a complete micro-experience rather than only saying “read a book.” Examples include:

- a five-minute explanation followed by one real-world observation;
- a short audio guide during an appropriate route;
- one concept applied while cooking or training;
- a local story connected to the place currently being visited;
- a small experiment, comparison, or question that can be completed away from the screen.

Long-form reading remains valid when the user asks for it, but it is one experience form among many.

## The Meaning Thread

An experience may contain a Meaning Thread with at most three parts:

1. **Before:** why this experience could matter now.
2. **During:** one to three optional insights or noticing cues.
3. **After:** one light question about what stayed with the person.

The Meaning Thread is optional. Presence wins when information would interrupt attention, safety, conversation, play, rest, or flow.

For the representative prototype, a Capsule contains at most three stage-specific guide moments. Preparation may preview their character, Presence surfaces only the current moment, and a deeper consultable guide may collect the rest. Memory may repeat at most two titles as an optional recognition cue, never as proof that learning occurred.

## Information quality

Every learning element declares its basis:

- verified editorial knowledge;
- current sourced data with provenance and freshness;
- local curator knowledge with attribution;
- generated synthesis, clearly labelled and grounded in sources;
- personal reflection, clearly belonging to the user.

If the system cannot support a claim, it asks a question, removes the insight, or stays silent. Fluency is never evidence.

## Where it lives in the product

- **Now:** the primary promise may connect to one current intention or meaning anchor, but remains concise.
- **Today:** opportunities may form a gentle rhythm around near intentions without filling every gap.
- **Discover:** the user may ask to learn, explore, understand, practice, or make progress in their own words.
- **Capsule:** guidance appears only at the stage where it helps the experience.
- **Life Book:** memories may reveal themes and growth over time without scores, streaks, or a prescribed life plan.
- **Profile / future direction editor:** the user can view, add, pause, change, and delete near intentions, growth directions, and meaning anchors.

No fifth primary navigation surface is approved by this document.

## Success test

The layer succeeds when someone can say:

- “I did something worthwhile.”
- “I understood a little more because I did it.”
- “This connected to something I care about.”

It fails when the user spends more time consuming explanations than living the experience, feels judged by a goal, or cannot distinguish fact from generated interpretation.
