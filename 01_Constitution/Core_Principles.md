# Core Principles

Status: Foundational  
Version: 1.0

These principles govern product, design, intelligence, and engineering decisions. Later documents may clarify them, but may not silently contradict them.

## 1. Life outside the screen is the outcome

Momentum exists to initiate meaningful real-world experience. Attention inside the app is a cost to minimize, not a metric to maximize.

## 2. One meaningful suggestion beats many adequate options

The primary experience presents the best current suggestion with honest uncertainty. Alternatives remain accessible without turning the product into an endless feed.

## 3. Promise before proof

People choose emotionally and validate rationally. First show what they may experience or feel. Then make “why this fits” available as concise, honest support.

## 4. Context should reduce effort, not remove agency

Context helps Momentum make a better proposal. It never gives Momentum authority over the user. The user can reject, override, refine, or independently explore.

## 5. Active intent complements passive understanding

Sometimes Momentum detects an opportunity. Sometimes the user knows something the system cannot know: “I have 30 minutes,” “I need calm,” or “I am with my family.” Both routes use the same understanding process.

## 6. Ask for the minimum context needed

More data is not inherently better. Momentum asks for access only when a direct benefit is clear, works meaningfully without optional permissions, and treats every permission as revocable.

## 7. Earn trust before requesting more

Trust creates willingness to share; useful context enables better suggestions; better suggestions can deepen trust. Momentum must never reverse this relationship by demanding data before demonstrating value.

## 8. Be honest about uncertainty

Momentum distinguishes observed facts, inferred context, and predictions. It explains a recommendation without pretending certainty or revealing sensitive detail unnecessarily.

## 9. The experience determines the interface

The interface adapts to the current human moment. A walk, recipe, workout, and recovery exercise may require different guidance, but share one experience language: only what is needed now.

## 10. Create momentum, then become quiet

The first seconds should turn interest into action. During the experience, guidance becomes minimal: a route, one cooking step, one exercise, a timer, or a subtle cue. Silence is a designed state.

## 11. Use existing capabilities well

Momentum orchestrates navigation, reminders, calendars, health data, weather, media, and device features where appropriate. It does not become a worse copy of a trusted platform capability.

## 12. Memories matter more than engagement

Reflection should be lightweight and humane. One feeling or one photo can be enough. Momentum preserves meaning without turning life into constant documentation.

## 13. Design for feeling without manipulation

Imagery, motion, language, and haptics may create desire and wonder, but must represent the likely experience truthfully. Momentum inspires; it does not exploit anxiety, guilt, scarcity, or addiction.

## 14. Simplicity is disciplined completeness

Simple does not mean empty. Show the minimum complete set of information needed to desire, trust, and begin the experience.

## 15. Every addition must name the human moment it improves

Before a feature enters design or development, its proposal must state:

1. Which human moment does it improve?
2. Which part of the core loop does it strengthen?
3. How does it lower mental effort or screen time?
4. What is the smallest complete version?

If these questions have no clear answers, the feature is not ready.
