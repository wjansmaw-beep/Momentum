# Blueprint 001 — An Unexpected Free Hour

Status: Directional product hypothesis  
Version: 1.0

## Why this moment

An appointment is cancelled or the person finishes early. Around one hour becomes available unexpectedly. This is a common point at which people may default to scrolling because the opening is valuable but too small or uncertain to plan well.

This blueprint tests Momentum's central promise:

> Can the product understand an unplanned opening and reveal one experience that feels more worthwhile than filling the time by habit?

It deliberately does not begin with a walk, workout, recipe, or destination.

## Entry routes

### Active entry

The user opens Momentum and says:

> I unexpectedly have about an hour.

This is the preferred first-product route because availability and willingness are explicit.

### Passive entry

Momentum notices a newly open calendar window only if the user has granted calendar access and chosen a bounded level of initiative. A gap alone does not prove availability or willingness.

Possible wording:

> It looks like some time opened up. Is that really yours?

The user can confirm, dismiss, or prevent similar prompts.

## Moment expiry

The moment expires when:

- the user states a new commitment;
- calendar or travel constraints materially change;
- too little time remains for the selected experience and return buffer;
- location or company changes;
- the user closes the opportunity with "not now."

## Context layers

### Explicit

- approximately one hour is available;
- willingness to receive a suggestion;
- any correction such as "at work," "stay nearby," "with my child," or "I want calm."

### Observed when permitted

- current time and approximate place;
- verified free/busy boundary;
- travel reach and current weather;
- relevant opening hours or fresh Living World opportunities;
- platform-supported capacity signals, used cautiously.

### Learned and editable

- interests and desired feelings;
- accessibility and practical constraints;
- familiar equipment or places;
- recent repetition and past felt outcomes.

### Unknown unless provided

- whether the person truly wants activity;
- whether they may leave their current place;
- current company;
- inner emotional state;
- whether an inferred recovery or energy need is correct.

## Progressive clarification

The smallest default input is the time window. Momentum uses permitted context to avoid unnecessary questions.

If one answer would materially change the candidate field, show a compact active-intent sheet:

> What do you have room for?

- **A feeling:** calm, energy, wonder, connection, challenge, surprise;
- **a boundary:** stay here, nearby, outdoors, no equipment, low cost;
- **company:** alone or together, only when relevant.

The user may skip this. Skipping means Momentum stays less specific, not that it invents an answer.

## Opportunity statement

> There is room for one complete, low-friction experience with enough buffer to return without rushing.

This sentence remains activity-neutral.

## Candidate field

The actual candidates depend on live context. The following illustrates the required breadth rather than a fixed set.

| Candidate form | Possible felt outcome | Strong reason for | Strong reason against |
|---|---|---|---|
| Short strength session | challenge, pride, energy | on-site equipment and clear duration | unsuitable when equipment, readiness, or changing time is missing |
| Nearby nature experience | wonder, spaciousness, restoration | favorable conditions and a meaningful nearby detail | travel and return buffer may consume too much of the hour |
| Brief cultural visit | curiosity, surprise | verified event or place already nearby | opening, ticketing, or visit length may be uncertain |
| Simple food experience | nourishment, enjoyment | meal timing and ingredients make it immediately useful | inventory may be unknown or preparation may exceed the window |
| Breathing or quiet recovery | calm, restoration | almost no setup and works in many places | may not match a request for challenge or discovery |
| Connection moment | warmth, connection | an explicitly desired shared opportunity | availability of another person must not be assumed |
| Conscious pause | relief, autonomy | filling time is not always necessary | should never be framed as failure or optimization |

## Hard filters

Every candidate must fit:

> preparation + travel out + experience + travel back + transition buffer ≤ remaining window

Remove candidates when:

- access, conditions, or opening cannot be verified;
- required equipment or permission is unavailable;
- a stated boundary is violated;
- known accessibility or safety needs are unmet;
- the capsule cannot guide the experience completely enough;
- a live claim is stale;
- commercial influence is the reason it appears.

## Comparative scenario A — At work, challenge requested

### Moment

- 58 minutes remain;
- the user says they can train at work;
- a challenge is explicitly desired;
- known equipment: one kettlebell;
- leaving the site would add uncertainty.

### Viable candidates

1. a 30-minute kettlebell session plus setup and return buffer;
2. a bodyweight interval requiring less equipment;
3. a brisk outdoor loop if access and conditions are verified;
4. a quiet reset, feasible but conflicting with explicit desired state.

### Selection hypothesis

The kettlebell session leads because explicit intent, feasibility, familiarity, and capsule readiness align. It does not lead because exercise is globally healthier or because Momentum wants activity.

### Experience Promise

> One kettlebell, a demanding half hour, and enough time to return without rushing. This could be the strong finish your unexpected break allows.

Compact commitment:

- 4 minutes to prepare;
- 30 minutes training;
- 6 minutes to change or settle;
- 18 minutes protected buffer.

Primary action: **Set up this session**  
Secondary action: **Something else fits me better**

### Capsule

Equipment confirmation → brief warm-up → one exercise or interval at a time → quiet timer and haptics → clear finish → optional one-tap felt outcome.

Momentum remains present only because sequence and timing are part of execution.

## Comparative scenario B — Near nature, no desired state supplied

### Moment

- 64 minutes remain near home;
- approximate location and dry weather are available;
- a verified quiet viewpoint is within practical reach;
- the user often values nature, but has not stated a present need;
- a strength session and a simple meal are also feasible.

### Selection behavior

The system may choose the nearby nature experience only when its distinctive current value and full time fit clearly exceed the alternatives. It uses modest language because the desired state is inferred.

### Experience Promise

> The light should reach the water before your next commitment. There is enough time to reach the quiet side of the path and be back without hurrying.

The Wonder detail must be sourced and current. Momentum does not promise wildlife, silence, or emotional recovery.

Primary action: **Take me there**  
Correction: **I would rather stay here**

### Capsule

Essential clothing or item → full time commitment → route handoff → one arrival cue → Momentum becomes silent → optional later reflection.

## Comparative scenario C — Low context

### Known

- the user explicitly has 45 minutes;
- no optional location, calendar, health, or inventory permissions are available.

### Response

Momentum must still be useful. It can ask one high-value question:

> Do you want to feel calmer, more energized, or pleasantly surprised?

It then selects from permission-light experiences that can be completed wherever the person is, or lets the user say "none of these."

It does not pressure the user to unlock better recommendations by granting access.

## Ranking explanation

The user-facing "Why this fits" should reveal only the few reasons needed for trust:

- fits the time including a return buffer;
- respects the stated place or equipment boundary;
- matches the desired feeling;
- uses one current, verified opportunity when relevant.

It should not expose calendar titles, private health detail, inferred relationships, or an artificial numerical score.

## Agency and redirection

The primary promise is one editorial choice, not a command. The user can:

- accept;
- say "not now";
- change the feeling or boundary;
- request one other direction.

Redirection re-runs selection with the correction. It does not open an endless content feed and does not debate the user's choice.

## Failure and changed conditions

If the weather changes, a place closes, equipment is unavailable, or too little time remains:

1. acknowledge the specific change;
2. preserve the user's desired outcome and constraints;
3. offer one feasible fallback or stop;
4. never pretend the original promise remains valid.

## Presence and Memory

Presence differs by selected experience:

- workout: minimal sequence and timing may remain active;
- route-based experience: a trusted navigation capability takes over;
- breathing: screen dims and cues become subtle;
- recipe: only the current step and timer remain;
- pause: Momentum disappears completely.

Afterward, one optional question may be enough:

> Was this a good use of the hour?

The response updates this kind of moment cautiously. It does not redefine the person's identity.

## What this blueprint proves

The blueprint succeeds when one input—unexpectedly available time—can lead to meaningfully different experiences depending on the person and present context, while:

- the activity remains an outcome;
- the recommendation is understandable;
- optional permissions improve rather than unlock the product;
- the user can correct the system in one gesture or sentence;
- the complete experience fits the real window;
- Momentum becomes quiet after creating momentum.

## Open design questions

- Is time alone enough for the first interaction, or should one desired feeling always be invited?
- Should the first prototype use active entry only to avoid premature proactive assumptions?
- How should "another direction" appear without weakening the primary promise?
- Which three permission-light candidates are strong enough for the first prototype?
- Which selected outcome best demonstrates handoff and Presence without biasing the product toward outdoor discovery?
